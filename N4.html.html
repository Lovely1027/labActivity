Let's study N4 level!

What is N4 Level?

N4 is the second level of the Japanese Language Proficiency Test (JLPT), just above N5. It is still considered a beginner to intermediate level, but it requires a deeper understanding of Japanese grammar, vocabulary, and kanji compared to N5. At this level, learners can handle more complex sentences and understand basic Japanese used in everyday life, especially in contexts that involve daily conversations, some work-related scenarios, and familiar topics.

Key Characteristics of the N4 Level:

Vocabulary:

  • At the N4 level, learners are expected to know around 1,500 words. These words cover a wider range of everyday topics compared to N5 and include words that are used in daily life, at work, and in more detailed conversations.

Examples of N4 vocabulary include:

  1. 速い (はやい, hayai) — "fast"
  2. 楽しい (たのしい, tanoshii) — "fun"
  3. 会話 (かいわ, kaiwa) — "conversation"
  4. 図書館 (としょかん, toshokan) — "library"
  5. 料理 (りょうり, ryouri) — "cooking" or "dish"

Grammar:

  • N4 learners should be familiar with more complex grammar structures than N5. This includes both basic and intermediate grammar patterns that allow for more nuanced and detailed expressions.

Some examples of N4 grammar points include:

  1. ている (te iru) — Used to describe actions in progress or habitual actions.
    Example: 勉強している (べんきょうしている, benkyou shiteiru) — "I am studying."

  2. ～ことがある (koto ga aru) — To express that something has happened at least once or occurs occasionally.
    Example: 日本に行ったことがある (にほんにいったことがある, Nihon ni itta koto ga aru) — "I have been to Japan."

  3. ～なければならない (nakereba naranai) — Must, have to.
    Example: 勉強しなければならない (べんきょうしなければならない, benkyou shinakereba naranai) — "I have to study."

  4. ～てもいい (temo ii) — It’s okay to do (giving permission).
    Example: ここに座ってもいいですか? (ここにすわってもいいですか? Koko ni suwatte mo ii desu ka?) — "Is it okay if I sit here?"

Kanji:

At N4, learners are expected to know around 300 Kanji characters, including many that are commonly used in daily life. These Kanji may appear in newspapers, books, and other types of media targeted toward people with basic to intermediate knowledge of Japanese.

Examples of N4 Kanji include:

  1. 時 (とき, toki) — "time" or "hour"
  2. 自 (じ, ji) — "self" or "own"
  3. 気 (き, ki) — "spirit," "mood," or "energy"
  4. 病 (びょう, byou) — "illness"
  5. 食 (しょく, shoku) — "eat," "food"

Listening:

  • N4 learners should be able to understand spoken Japanese in more everyday contexts. This includes simple conversations about familiar topics, such as shopping, travel, and family life.
  • They should be able to understand basic Japanese spoken at a normal speed in clear and structured speech. However, they may still have difficulty understanding fast, casual conversations or technical language.
  • Example scenario: Listening to a conversation where someone talks about what they did over the weekend, and being able to pick out the key details.

Reading:

  • At N4, learners can read short passages about familiar topics. These passages may appear in written forms like emails, messages, or simple narratives. N4-level reading also includes basic signs or advertisements.
  • N4 learners will be able to read short stories, instructions, and basic news articles or blog posts, although they may still need a dictionary for more advanced Kanji or vocabulary.
  • Example reading material: A letter asking about someone’s weekend plans, or a short article about a famous landmark in Japan.

Writing:

  • At N4, learners can write simple sentences and short paragraphs using basic grammar and vocabulary.
  • They should be able to write short notes or letters, fill out forms, and write about familiar topics such as hobbies, family, or plans.
  • Example: Writing a short letter telling a friend about your last holiday or what you plan to do this weekend.

JLPT N4 Test Structure:

The JLPT N4 test consists of three main sections:

Language Knowledge (Vocabulary and Grammar):

  • Measures your understanding of vocabulary and grammar.
  • You’ll be tested on both vocabulary and grammar that you can apply in reading and listening.

Reading:

  • Tests your ability to read and understand written Japanese.
  • You’ll be asked to answer questions based on short passages or dialogues.

Listening:

  • Measures your ability to understand spoken Japanese.
  • The listening section involves conversations or dialogues about everyday situations. You’ll need to understand the key points, details, and intentions of the speakers.

Example Sentences for N4 Learners:

  1. この店は安いです (このみせはやすいです, Kono mise wa yasui desu) — "This store is cheap."
  2. 昨日は雨でした (きのうはあめでした, Kinou wa ame deshita) — "It rained yesterday."
  3. 明日、友達と映画を見に行きます (あした、ともだちとえいがをみにいきます, Ashita, tomodachi to eiga o mini ikimasu) — "Tomorrow, I will go watch a movie with my friend."

Study Tips for N4:

  1. Review N5 content: Make sure you have a solid foundation in N5 before tackling N4 material.

  2. Textbooks: Use textbooks such as Genki II, Minna no Nihongo II, or Tae Kim's Guide to Japanese Grammar.

  3. Kanji: Study the Kanji systematically, and use resources like WaniKani or Anki to help with memorization.

  4. Practice: Try listening to Japanese podcasts, watching anime with subtitles, or reading manga or news articles aimed at beginners.

  5. Mock Exams: Take practice exams or quizzes to get used to the test format and time constraints.


At the N4 level, you have moved beyond the absolute basics of Japanese and are starting to engage with more complex conversations, reading, and writing. While you may still find some areas challenging, you should be able to communicate more confidently in everyday situations and continue to build your foundation for more advanced study.
