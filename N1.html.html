Let's study N1 level!

What is N1 Level?

  • N1 is the highest level of the Japanese Language Proficiency Test (JLPT). At this level, learners are expected to have advanced proficiency in Japanese.
  • N1 represents fluency or near-native proficiency, and it is typically for those who wish to use Japanese in professional, academic, or high-level social settings.
  • Passing the N1 test means that you can handle almost any type of communication in Japanese, including complex readings, high-level conversations, and nuanced writing. You’ll have a deep understanding of grammar, vocabulary, Kanji, and expressions used in formal, literary, technical, and informal contexts.

Key Characteristics of the N1 Level:

Vocabulary:

  • N1 learners are expected to know approximately (10,000 words). This includes advanced vocabulary that spans a variety of topics like economics, science, culture, and history.
  • The vocabulary covers a wide range of formal and colloquial words, and learners must be able to comprehend and use these words in complex contexts.

Examples of N1 vocabulary include:

  1. 解決 (かいけつ, kaiketsu) — "solution"
  2. 顕著 (けんちょ, kencho) — "remarkable," "significant"
  3. 不明 (ふめい, fumei) — "unclear," "unknown"
  4. 迅速 (じんそく, jinsoku) — "prompt," "quick"
  5. 反映 (はんえい, han'ei) — "reflection" (as in, a result or influence)

Grammar

  • At N1, learners should be familiar with high-level grammar and able to use it with precision. This includes the ability to express abstract concepts, convey subtle nuances, and understand advanced sentence structures.
  • The grammar used at this level can be very formal, and learners are expected to master difficult expressions often used in literature, news media, politics, and business settings.

Examples of N1 grammar points:

  1. ～をもって (wo motte) — "with," "by means of" (formal, often used in written language)
    - この問題はその専門家をもって解決されるべきだ。 (このもんだいはそのせんもんかをもってかいけつされるべきだ, Kono mondai wa sono senmonka o motte kaiketsu sareru beki da)
    — "This problem should be solved by experts in the field."

  2. ～にもかかわらず (ni mo kakawarazu) — "despite," "in spite of"
    - 彼は年齢にもかかわらず、非常にエネルギッシュだ (かれはねんれいにもかかわらず、ひじょうにエネルギッシュだ, Kare wa nenrei ni mo kakawarazu, hijō ni enerugisshuda)
    — "Despite his age, he is very energetic."

  3. ～いかんによっては (ikan ni yotte wa) — "depending on (the situation)"
    - この計画は結果いかんによっては、変更されるかもしれない (このけいかくはけっかいかんによっては、へんこうされるかもしれない, Kono keikaku wa kekka ikan ni yotte wa, henkō sareru kamoshirenai)
    — "This plan may change depending on the results."

Kanji

  • N1 learners are expected to know approximately 2,000 Kanji. These Kanji characters are used in a wide range of texts, from literature to business documents, academic papers, and newspapers.
  • You will encounter rare readings, complex Kanji compounds, and formal expressions that are not found at lower levels.

Examples of N1-level Kanji include:

  1. - 誠 (せい, sei) — "sincerity," "truth"
  2. - 概念 (がいねん, gainen) — "concept," "notion"
  3. - 無理 (むり, muri) — "impossible," "unreasonable"
  4. - 機構 (きこう, kikō) — "organization," "mechanism"
  5. - 複雑 (ふくざつ, fukuzatsu) — "complex," "complicated"

Listening

  • N1 listening comprehension tests your ability to understand fast-paced, natural speech in various situations, including formal speeches, academic discussions, debates, and high-level business meetings.
  • You’ll be expected to grasp abstract concepts, indirect expressions, and nuanced meanings in spoken Japanese.
  • Example scenario: Understanding a political debate on TV, a scientific lecture, or a formal business meeting discussing financial results.

Reading

  • N1 learners can read and understand complex texts that include both literal and figurative language. This includes newspapers, magazines, literary works, legal texts, and academic papers.
  • You should be able to analyze complex arguments, understand abstract ideas, and interpret nuanced meanings.
  • Example reading material: Research articles in academic journals, in-depth news articles on current events, and literary works that involve metaphor and symbolism.

Writing

  • At N1, learners are expected to produce high-quality writing that uses advanced vocabulary, grammar, and Kanji. You should be able to express abstract ideas, construct detailed arguments, and organize complex ideas in both formal and informal settings.
  • Example: Writing an academic paper, a formal business report, or a thoughtful opinion essay on a complex issue.

JLPT N1 Test Structure:

The JLPT N1 test consists of three main sections:

Language Knowledge (Vocabulary and Grammar):

  • This section evaluates your ability to understand advanced-level grammar and vocabulary. It tests your ability to use complex grammar in context and understand nuanced vocabulary in various situations.

Reading:

  • This section tests your ability to read and comprehend complex written texts. The reading material includes newspaper articles, literary excerpts, technical writings, and in-depth opinion pieces.

Listening:

  • The listening section assesses your ability to understand natural, rapid, and complex spoken Japanese in a variety of contexts, such as formal speeches, academic discussions, and daily conversations with advanced vocabulary.

Example Sentences for N1 Learners:

  1. この問題を解決するには、複雑な要素を考慮に入れる必要がある (このもんだいをかいけつするには、ふくざつなようそをこうりょにいれるひつようがある, Kono mondai o kaiketsu suru ni wa, fukuzatsu na yōso o kōryo ni ireru hitsuyō ga aru)
    — "In order to solve this problem, it is necessary to take into account the complex factors."

  2. 彼の提案は非常に革新的であり、我々の業界に大きな影響を与えるだろう (かれのていあんはひじょうにかくしんてきであり、われわれのぎょうかいにおおきなえいきょうをあたえるだろう, Kare no teian wa hijō ni kakushinteki de ari, wareware no gyōkai ni ōkina eikyō o ataeru darō)
    — "His proposal is very innovative and will likely have a significant impact on our industry."

  3. 最近、彼の発言は明らかに政治的な意味を持つようになってきた (さいきん、かれのはつげんはあきらかにせいじてきないみをもつようになってきた, Saikin, kare no hatsugen wa akiraka ni seijiteki na imi o motsu yō ni natte kita)
    — "Recently, his remarks have clearly begun to carry political implications."

Study Tips for N1:

  1. Master N2 material: Make sure you’re comfortable with N2 before progressing to N1. N1 builds on the foundation you’ve laid at N2.

  2. Advanced Textbooks: Use textbooks like "Sou Matome N1* or "Nihongo So-Matome", which are specifically tailored to the N1 level.

  3. Kanji Practice: Consistently review and expand your knowledge of Kanji. Use tools like WaniKani, Anki, or Kanji books to ensure you’re familiar with all 2,000 Kanji.

  4. Listening Practice: Listen to news broadcasts, **lectures, political discussions, and advanced podcasts to practice understanding rapid, sophisticated Japanese.

  5. Read Widely: Regularly read newspapers, literary works, academic texts, and non-fiction books. Focus on articles that involve complex subject matter.

  6. Practice Writing: Work on writing essays, formal letters, and reports. Ensure your writing includes advanced vocabulary, complex grammar, and proper Kanji usage.

At the N1 level, you can confidently understand native-level content in a variety of settings, whether academic, professional, or social. You are able to communicate with ease and nuance, read high-level texts, and write advanced compositions. Achieving N1 is a strong indicator of fluency in Japanese and opens up opportunities for working or studying in Japan at a professional or academic level.
