Let's study N3 level!

What is N3 Level?

N3 is the third level of the Japanese Language Proficiency Test (JLPT), positioned between N4 (beginner-intermediate) and N2 (advanced-intermediate). At the N3 level, learners are expected to have intermediate proficiency in Japanese. They can handle more complex grammar, vocabulary, and Kanji, and understand everyday conversations on a wider range of topics, though they may still face challenges with nuanced or specialized content.

Key Characteristics of the N3 Level:

Vocabulary:

N3 learners are expected to know around 3,750 words. This includes a broader range of vocabulary compared to N4, including words related to more advanced everyday situations such as work, travel, and personal experiences.

Examples of N3 vocabulary include:

  1. 意見 (いけん, iken) — "opinion"
  2. 複雑 (ふくざつ, fukuzatsu) — "complicated"
  3. 目的 (もくてき, mokuteki) — "purpose"
  4. 確か (たしか, tashika) — "certain" or "sure"
  5. 練習 (れんしゅう, renshū) — "practice"

Grammar:

N3 grammar introduces more complex structures that are used in various everyday and conversational contexts. Learners should be able to comprehend and use more nuanced grammar to express themselves in both spoken and written Japanese.

Examples of N3 grammar:

  1. ～ことがある (koto ga aru)
    —"have done (something) before" (experience)

  2. 日本に行ったことがある (にほんにいったことがある, Nihon ni itta koto ga aru)
    — "I have been to Japan."

  3. ～ようになる (yō ni naru)
    — "to come to" (something that has changed, or a process)

  4. 日本語が話せるようになった (にほんごがはなせるようになった, Nihongo ga hanaseru yō ni natta)
    — "I’ve come to be able to speak Japanese."

  5. ～たり～たりする (tari tari suru)
    — "doing things like (this) and (that)" (listing actions)

  6. 映画を見たり、本を読んだりします (えいがをみたり、ほんをよんだりします, Eiga o mitari, hon o yondari shimasu)
    — "I do things like watch movies and read books."

  7. ～たらどうですか (tara dō desu ka)
    — "How about doing (something)?"

  8. 休んだらどうですか (やすんだらどうですか, Yasundara dō desu ka)
    — "How about taking a rest?"

Kanji:

At the N3 level, learners should know around 650 Kanji. This includes more frequent and advanced Kanji, such as those used in newspapers, novels, and formal writing. You’ll start to encounter more complex Kanji compounds (words that are made up of two or more Kanji characters).

Examples of N3-level Kanji include:

  1. 環境 (かんきょう, kankyō) — "environment"
  2. 実現 (じつげん, jitsugen) — "realization"
  3. 経験 (けいけん, keiken) — "experience"
  4. 成長 (せいちょう, seichō) — "growth"
  5. 普段 (ふだん, fudan) — "usual, everyday"

Listening:

  • Listening comprehension at N3 involves understanding conversations on more varied topics, including news reports, interviews, and daily life. Learners can follow conversations where the speed is slightly faster, and the content is more contextually rich.
  • N3 learners should be able to understand intermediate-level spoken Japanese in natural conversations, but they may still struggle with specialized or unfamiliar topics.
  • Example scenario: Understanding an announcement about a public event, a conversation about past experiences, or a news story about a local event.

Reading:

  • N3 learners can read and understand texts with more complex content, including articles, short stories, simple essays, and personal letters. They can understand basic news articles, and messages that include slightly technical or abstract language.
  • They should be able to grasp the general meaning of a text, but they might struggle with advanced Kanji or specialized terminology.
  • Example reading material: A letter from a friend describing their work, a newspaper article discussing a popular cultural event, or a simple story with a bit of nuance.

Writing:

  • At N3, learners can write short essays, emails, or letters on a variety of topics. They can express simple opinions, descriptions, or stories, but may struggle with more formal or complex writing.
  • Example: Writing a letter to a friend about a trip, or describing your daily routine and experiences.

JLPT N3 Test Structure:

The JLPT N3 test consists of three main sections:

Language Knowledge (Vocabulary and Grammar):

  • This section tests your understanding of vocabulary and grammar.
  • You will need to identify the correct usage of vocabulary and grammar points in context, as well as choose the appropriate expressions for different situations.

Reading:

  • This section tests your ability to read and comprehend written texts at an intermediate level.
  • The reading material will consist of short passages, and you will be required to answer questions based on comprehension and the meaning of the text.

Listening:

  • This section measures your ability to understand spoken Japanese.
  • The listening section will involve conversations, announcements, and dialogues in everyday situations at a moderate pace. You will be tested on your understanding of the main ideas and specific details.

Example Sentences for N3 Learners:

  1. 最近忙しいです (さいきん いそがしい です, Saikin isogashii desu)
    — "I’ve been busy recently."

  2. 仕事が終わったら、友達と会います (しごとがおわったら、ともだちとあいます, Shigoto ga owattara, tomodachi to aimasu)
    — "When work finishes, I will meet my friends."

  3. 彼は何かを探しているようです (かれはなにかをさがしているようです, Kare wa nanika o sagashite iru yō desu)
    — "He seems to be looking for something."

Study Tips for N3:

  1. Review N4 content: Ensure you have mastered the N4 level before tackling N3. Understanding the basics will make the jump to N3 easier.

  2. Practice with N3-level materials: Use textbooks like "Genki II" or "Minna no Nihongo II" to study more intermediate grammar and vocabulary.

  3. Kanji Practice: Use tools like WaniKani or Anki to study and memorize N3 Kanji systematically.

  4. Listening practice: Listen to Japanese podcasts, watch news videos or dramas, and use apps like JLPT Sensei or Nihongo-Pro to practice listening comprehension.

  5. Read Japanese regularly: Start reading short articles, blogs, and manga to improve your reading comprehension.

  6. Use flashcards: Use flashcards to memorize vocabulary, grammar, and Kanji effectively.

At the N3 level, you can understand and express yourself in intermediate-level Japanese. You can handle everyday conversations, read simple texts with some complex grammar, and write basic letters or essays. It's a crucial level for building a strong foundation for the higher levels of JLPT (N2 and N1). If you can achieve N3, you'll be well-equipped to handle most of the Japanese you encounter in daily life, including in work and social contexts.
