Let's Study Hiragana!

What is Hiragana?

Hiragana (ひらがな) is one of the three main writing systems used in the Japanese language, alongside katakana and kanji. Hiragana is a phonetic alphabet, meaning each character represents a specific syllable or sound. It is primarily used for native Japanese words, grammatical elements, and particles. Hiragana characters are often used to write words that do not have kanji or when kanji are too difficult or obscure.

Key Features of Hiragana:

1. Syllabic System: Each character represents a syllable, not an individual sound or letter. For example, か (ka), き (ki), く (ku), け (ke), and こ (ko) are the five basic sounds of the "k" row in hiragana.

2. Used for Function Words: Hiragana is often used to write grammatical elements like particles (e.g., は [wa], を [wo], に [ni]), verb endings (e.g., ます [masu]), and adjectives.

3. Pronunciation: The characters are pronounced based on the sounds of the Japanese language, with some exceptions for variations in pitch or inflection.

4. Learning Order: Hiragana is generally taught before katakana and kanji, as it represents the basic building blocks of Japanese phonetics.

Example:

  1. こんにちは (konnichiwa) – "Hello"
  2. さようなら (sayōnara) – "Goodbye"
  3. ありがとう (arigatou) – "Thank you"

There are 46 basic hiragana characters, along with additional combinations (like きゃ [kya], しゅ [shu]) created by adding diacritical marks called dakuten (ﾞ) and handakuten (ﾟ).
