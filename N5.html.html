Let's study N5 level!

What is N5 Level?

N5 is the beginner level of the Japanese Language Proficiency Test (JLPT), a standardized test used to evaluate and certify the Japanese language proficiency of non-native speakers. The JLPT has five levels in total, from N1 (the highest) to N5 (the lowest). N5 is the easiest level and typically corresponds to someone who has just started learning Japanese.

Basic Vocabulary:

At the N5 level, learners are expected to know approximately 800 words. These words include simple, everyday vocabulary that is commonly used in basic conversations, such as numbers, time expressions, common greetings, and names of places or foods.

Examples of N5 vocabulary include:

  1. ありがとう (arigatou) — "thank you"
  2. おはよう (ohayou) — "good morning"
  3. 学校 (gakkou) — "school"
  4. 猫 (neko) — "cat"

Basic Grammar:

Learners should be familiar with the basic structure of Japanese sentences and common grammatical patterns. These include:

  1. Simple sentence structures: Subject-Object-Verb (SOV), like "I eat apple" → 私はりんごを食べます (Watashi wa ringo o tabemasu).
  2. Particles like は (wa), が (ga), を (o), and に (ni), which are used to mark the subject, object, or direction of a sentence.
  3. Basic verb conjugations, including the present and past tense of polite verbs (like 食べます - tabemasu for "eat" in the present, and 食べました - tabemashita for "ate" in the past).

Hiragana and Katakana:

  • At the N5 level, learners are expected to have mastered Hiragana and Katakana, the two basic Japanese scripts.
  • They should be able to read and write simple words, phrases, and sentences in both Hiragana and Katakana, as well as recognize basic Kanji (around 100 characters).

Kanji:

  • Learners should know around 100 basic Kanji characters at the N5 level. These Kanji are common, everyday characters used in simple words.

Examples of N5-level Kanji include:

  1. 日 (hi, nichi) — "day" or "sun"
  2. 人 (hito) — "person"
  3. 学 (gaku) — "study" or "learning"
  4. 年 (nen) — "year"

Listening:

  • At the N5 level, learners should be able to understand basic conversations about everyday topics, such as greetings, introductions, shopping, or ordering food, especially if spoken slowly and clearly.
  • For example, they may understand simple questions like "What is your name?" (お名前は何ですか? / Onamae wa nan desu ka?) and basic responses.

Reading:

  • N5 learners should be able to read simple texts such as short messages, advertisements, or signs. These texts will mostly use Hiragana, Katakana, and a small number of Kanji.
  • The texts will usually involve everyday situations and familiar topics (e.g., food, family, hobbies, school).

What N5 Exam Focuses On:

The JLPT N5 test consists of three sections:

  1. Language Knowledge (Vocabulary & Grammar) — Measures your ability to understand and use basic words and grammar.
  2. Reading — Tests your ability to understand simple written texts.
  3. Listening — Assesses your understanding of spoken Japanese in basic everyday situations.

Study Materials for N5:

To prepare for the JLPT N5, you can use a variety of resources:

  • Textbooks such as "Genki I" or "Minna no Nihongo" are popular choices.
  • Flashcards (e.g., Anki) to help memorize vocabulary and Kanji.
  • Practice exams to familiarize yourself with the test format and timing.
  • Online courses or language apps like Duolingo, WaniKani, and LingoDeer.

Example N5 Sentence:

  1. 私は学生です (Watashi wa gakusei desu) — "I am a student."
  2. 私 (Watashi) = "I"
  3. は (wa) = Topic marker
  4. 学生 (gakusei) = "student"
  5. です (desu) = Polite sentence ending

Achieving N5 proficiency means you have a basic understanding of the Japanese language. You can understand and use simple phrases, read basic texts, and participate in basic conversations. It is a foundation level, and you would need to study further to progress to higher levels like N4 and beyond, where the complexity of vocabulary, grammar, and Kanji increases.
